# Interrogate Idiom

Use this when the durable unit is not the argument but the phrase, move, or distinction carrying the force.

The goal is idiom mining: find or coin a compact language-form that can travel, recur, become a touchstone, and train shared attention.

In the Center Study sense, an idiom is not just style. It is a verbal unit that becomes usable currency inside a scene: it orients attention, carries debt to the center, and becomes more true/useful as people repeat, exchange, and act through it.

## Center Study Source

Primary reference: Dennis Bouvard, [On Credit and the Idiom](https://www.centersearchcenter.com/2024-12-20/on-credit-and-the-idiom).

Working maxim: idioms now need to be manufactured rather than inherited; good idioms are paradoxical, imperative, iterable, and gain force through repetition.

## Rule

Do not paraphrase the idiom into bland explanation until you know what pressure it compresses and what scene it could organize.

## Questions

### 1. Locate the idiom

1. What exact phrase, image, distinction, or move is carrying the force?
2. Is it already stated, or must it be coined from the note?
3. What plain-language claim would lose force if substituted for it?
4. Is it compact enough to be carried, repeated, or remembered?

### 2. Locate the scene

5. What scene, conflict, mimetic pressure, or repeated failure produced the need for this idiom?
6. What does the idiom defer, crystallize, or make speakable?
7. What does it ask people to attend to together?
8. What older phrase or inherited frame is it remixing, remedying, remembering, or rejecting?

### 3. Locate the compression

9. What distinction does the idiom compress?
10. What does it let the reader notice that a normal explanation misses?
11. What does it make newly easy to say, test, or refuse?
12. Does it have paradoxical or imperative force?

### 4. Test fluency

13. Would the phrase look random from outside the idiom-space but increasingly necessary inside use?
14. What practice, repeated scene, or shared problem would make someone fluent in it?
15. What would it mean to "get" the idiom?
16. What would it make self-evident after repeated use?

### 5. Test exchange

17. What future exchanges could this idiom enable?
18. What makes it redeemable rather than decorative?
19. Does it work as credit, cash, named encounter, anonymous exchange, or team shorthand?
20. What would make it collapse into mere cleverness?

### 6. Apply it

21. If this idiom is right, what should we start noticing?
22. What should we stop saying?
23. What question does it make unavoidable?
24. Is it a private shorthand, a team touchstone, a poster, a publishable frame, or a skill/rule?

## Guardrail

If the phrase only sounds good inside one note, it is not an idiom yet. It is wording.

If the phrase cannot be repeated into greater force, exchanged in future scenes, or used to train attention, do not promote it. Keep mining.
